2. Current situation and trend estimation¶

2.1 Simplified model of R0¶

In epidemiology, the basic reproduction number (R0) of an infection can be thought of as the expected number of cases directly generated by one case.

  • If R0 > 1, each existing infection causes more than one new infection. The disease will spread between population and cause an outbreak.
  • If R0 = 1, each existing infection causes one new infection. The disease will stay alive but it no longer leads to an epidemic.
  • If R0 < 1, each existing infection causes less than one new infection. The disease will decline and eventually die out.

The R0 value only applies when everyone in a population is completely vulnerable to the disease, therefore all the goverment restriction measures(i.e. lockdown, social distancing) are to lower the R0 value until it's less than 1.

With fairly limited relevant knowledge and not further detailed dataset, I will use a very crude and inaccurate model to calculate the reproductive rates, just to show the numerical growth trend of confirmed cases and deaths:

  • If we assume every infected people will infect R0 people during one day, we then have:

    $$R_0 \ of \ the \ day \ = \ \frac{cumulative \ cases \ of \ the \ day}{cumulative \ cases \ of \ last \ day} \ - \ 1$$

We then plot the graph (from 2020-02-28):¶

In [7]:
uk1['R0Cases'] = uk1.CumCases / (uk1.CumCases - uk1.NewCases) - 1
uk1['R0Deaths'] = uk1.CumDeaths / (uk1.CumDeaths - uk1.NewDeaths) - 1
uk1.Date = pd.date_range('2020-1-25','2020-4-10',freq='D') 
uk1.set_index('Date',inplace=True)

plt.figure(figsize=(8,4))
plt.plot(uk1['2020-2-28':].R0Cases,label='R0 of infection')
plt.plot(uk1['2020-2-28':].R0Deaths,label='R0 of deaths')
plt.title('The R0 values of infection and deaths')
plt.legend()
plt.show()

Despite the simplicity of the formula, there's some information we can gain from this graph:

  1. The situation is getting better.
    • As the restrictive measures such as social distancing and lockdown were introduced, the both R0 values of infection and deaths are **gradually decreasing** . It does not mean that the number isn't growing rapidly but the rate of growing itself is slowing down.
    • The exact R0 values in the graph cannot be trusted, as I was assumning infection period to be 1 day while it's actually around 10 days (and many other vital factors I didn't take into consideration), the actually R0 value should be at least few times larger.
    • The graph of deaths was extremely unstable in the early March and it was due to the small sample size.
  2. We don't have enough tests.
    • We can see that the graph of deaths is **above the infection while they are supposed to be at the same level, it indicates that there are more** infected people than recorded and we're not testing enough.

2.2 Turning point judgement¶

In the middle of an exponential curve, it's very hard for us to tell how it will develop next or whether the turning point is approching. However, for an exponential function y = f(x), if we plot y in x-axis, and the changes of x value (i.e. delta x) in y-axis, both in logarithmic scale, the result graph should be linear.

This method has provided us a fairly straightforward idea to show the trend:

  • If we plot total number of cases in x-axis and new cases in y-axis (both in logarithmic scale), where the graph being **linear means that the virus is spreading, while a drop** in the graph will then indicate that the pandemic is under control.

We use the data in Italy as an example:¶

In [8]:
Italy = pd.read_csv('Italy_by_day.csv',parse_dates=['date'],index_col=['date'])
sns.regplot(x='cumulative_cases',y='daily_positive_cases',data=Italy,order=4).set(xscale='log',yscale='log')
plt.title('The turning point in Italy')
plt.show()

Italy is one of the countries most affected by the coronavirus and has the highest death rate at the time, with still hundreds of daily reported cases and a huge number of total cases. However, from the graph above we can see clearly that Italy is getting out of the crisis and the hardest time has passed, as the changing rate drops and it's no longer in exponential growth.

Now let's have a look at the data in the UK:¶

In [9]:
sns.regplot(x='CumCases',y='NewCases',data=uk1['2020-2-28':],order=4).set(xscale='log',yscale='log')
plt.title('The turning point in the UK')
plt.show()

Compared with Italy, the UK is facing more challenges at the moment. There's still no clear turning point observed in the graph yet, indicating that the development is still in line with the exponential law. However, at the end of the curve, we can see that the scattered points are more clustered, and the statistical algorithms also simulated a downward trend. Therefore I would estimate that we are approching the turning point (the peak), although there are still uncertainties.

To further improve the estimation, we can have more comparisons with Italy:¶

Given the information that:

  1. The turning point of Italy in the first graph happened on around March 25 2020.
  2. On March 25 2020, Italy had 4,492 new cases, 74,386 total cases and 7,503 total deaths.
  3. Now on April 10 2020, the UK has 5,195 new cases, 70,272 total cases and 8,958 total deaths.
  4. On April 8 2020, the number of new cases in Italy dropped below 1,000 for the first time and kept decreasing.

Combining the data and graphs above, and assuming the development of coronavirus follows a similar pattern in the UK and Italy, we have enough reason to beleive that the desease will reach a peak whitin the next few days in the UK, and daily new cases will decline below 1,000 within the next 25 days.

From this graph, we can tell that:

  1. Mortality rate increased almost exponentially at the beginning and is still growing.
  2. The growth rate of mortality has been much slower but not yet stable.
  3. The growth rate of infection has been much slower and fairly close to stable.

Therefore, one of the most important tasks at the moment is to lower the mortality rate.

3. Conlusion¶

I estimated the trend from three aspects in this report and the results are as follows:

  1. The restrictive measures effecively worked and we are now fairly close to the peak.
  2. The daily number of new cases is very likely to drop below 1,000 within the next 25 days.
  3. We haven't tested enough and the right thing to do now is to expand testing and lower the mortality rate.
